Chapter IX. The Army on the March435

1. Sun Tzǔ said: We come now to the question of encamping the army, and observing signs of the enemy.436 Pass quickly over mountains,437 and keep in the neighborhood of valleys.438

2. Camp in high places,439

facing the sun.440

Do not climb heights in order to fight.441 So much for mountain warfare.442

3. After crossing a river, you should get far away from it.443

4. When an invading force crosses a river in its onward march, do not advance to meet it in mid-stream. It will be best to let half the army get across, and then deliver your attack.444

5. If you are anxious to fight, you should not go to meet the invader near a river which he has to cross.445

6. Moor your craft higher up than the enemy, and facing the sun.446

Do not move up-stream to meet the enemy.447

So much for river warfare.

7. In crossing salt-marshes, your sole concern should be to get over them quickly, without any delay.448

8. If forced to fight in a salt-marsh, you should have water and grass near you, and get your back to a clump of trees.449

So much for operations in salt-marches.

9. In dry, level country, take up an easily accessible position450 with rising ground to your right and on your rear,451

so that the danger may be in front, and safety lie behind.452 So much for campaigning in flat country.

10. These are the four useful branches of military knowledge453

which enabled the Yellow Emperor to vanquish four several sovereigns.454

11. All armies prefer high ground to low.455

and sunny places to dark.

12. If you are careful of your men,456

and camp on hard ground,457 the army will be free from disease of every kind,458

and this will spell victory.

13. When you come to a hill or a bank, occupy the sunny side, with the slope on your right rear. Thus you will at once act for the benefit of your soldiers and utilize the natural advantages of the ground.

14. When, in consequence of heavy rains up-country, a river which you wish to ford is swollen and flecked with foam, you must wait until it subsides.459

15. Country in which there are precipitous cliffs with torrents running between,460 deep natural hollows,461

confined places,462

tangled thickets,463

quagmires464

and crevasses,465

should be left with all possible speed and not approached.

16. While we keep away from such places, we should get the enemy to approach them; while we face them, we should let the enemy have them on his rear.

17. If in the neighborhood of your camp466 there should be any hilly country,467 ponds surrounded by aquatic grass, hollow basins filled with reeds,468 or woods with thick undergrowth,469 they must be carefully routed out and searched; for these are places where men in ambush or insidious spies are likely to be lurking.470

18. When the enemy is close at hand and remains quiet, he is relying on the natural strength of his position.471

19. When he keeps aloof and tries to provoke a battle, he is anxious for the other side to advance.472

20. If his place of encampment is easy of access, he is tendering a bait.473

21. Movement amongst the trees of a forest shows that the enemy is advancing.474

The appearance of a number of screens in the midst of thick grass means that the enemy wants to make us suspicious.475

22. The rising of birds in their flight is the sign of an ambuscade.476

Startled beasts indicate that a sudden attack is coming.477

23. When there is dust rising in a high column, it is the sign of chariots advancing; when the dust is low, but spread over a wide area, it betokens the approach of infantry.478

When it branches out in different directions, it shows that parties have been sent to collect firewood.480 A few clouds of dust moving to and fro signify that the army is encamping.481

24. Humble words and increased preparations are signs that the enemy is about to advance.482

Violent language and driving forward as if to the attack are signs that he will retreat.483

25. When the light chariots484 come out first and take up a position on the wings, it is a sign that the enemy is forming for battle.485

26. Peace proposals unaccompanied by a sworn covenant indicate a plot.486

27. When there is much running about487

and the soldiers fall into rank,488 it means that the critical moment has come.489

28. When some are seen advancing and some retreating, it is a lure.490

29. When the soldiers stand leaning on their spears, they are faint from want of food.491

30. If those who are sent to draw water begin by drinking themselves, the army is suffering from thirst.492

31. If the enemy sees an advantage to be gained493 and makes no effort to secure it, the soldiers are exhausted.

32. If birds gather on any spot, it is unoccupied.494

Clamor by night betokens nervousness.495

33. If there is disturbance in the camp, the general’s authority is weak. If the banners and flags are shifted about, sedition is afoot.496 If the officers are angry, it means that the men are weary.497

34. When an army feeds its horses with grain and kills its cattle for food,498

and when the men do not hang their cooking-pots499 over the camp-fires,500 showing that they will not return to their tents, you may know that they are determined to fight to the death.501

35. The sight of men whispering together502 in small knots503 or speaking in subdued tones504 points to disaffection amongst the rank and file.505

36. Too frequent rewards signify that the enemy is at the end of his resources;506

too many punishments betray a condition of dire distress.507

37. To begin by bluster, but afterwards to take fright at the enemy’s numbers, shows a supreme lack of intelligence.508

38. When envoys are sent with compliments in their mouths, it is a sign that the enemy wishes for a truce.509

39. If the enemy’s troops march up angrily and remain facing ours for a long time without either joining battle or taking themselves off again, the situation is one that demands great vigilance and circumspection.510

40. If our troops are no more in number than the enemy, that is amply sufficient;511 it only means that no direct attack can be made.512

What we can do is simply to concentrate all our available strength, keep a close watch on the enemy, and obtain reinforcements.513

41. He who exercises no forethought but makes light of his opponents is sure to be captured by them.514

42. If soldiers are punished before they have grown attached to you, they will not prove submissive; and, unless submissive, then will be practically useless. If, when the soldiers have become attached to you, punishments are not enforced, they will still be useless.515

43. Therefore soldiers must be treated in the first instance with humanity, but kept under control by means of iron discipline.516

This is a certain road to victory.

44. If in training soldiers commands are habitually enforced, the army will be well-disciplined; if not, its discipline will be bad.517

45. If a general shows confidence in his men but always insists on his orders being obeyed,518

the gain will be mutual.519
